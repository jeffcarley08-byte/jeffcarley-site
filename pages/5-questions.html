The 5 Questions Every Leader Must Ask

After 4,500+ hours teaching at Airman Leadership School, I watched good leaders stall on the same five blind spots. These questions show you exactly where your leadership is strong — and where it isn’t.

Most leaders don’t fail because they lack intelligence or effort. They fail because they stop asking themselves hard questions. When you’re managing up, managing down, and putting out fires, it’s easy to stop examining the thing doing the examining: you.

These five questions are the ones I’ve returned to across four years of instruction and hundreds of leadership conversations. They don’t have easy answers. That’s the point. What you discover will tell you more than any personality test.

1
Question One

Do the people I lead trust me enough to tell me the truth?

This is the foundational question — and most leaders answer it wrong. They think trust means loyalty or agreement. It doesn’t. Trust means safety to tell you something you don’t want to hear.

If your team only tells you what’s going well, you don’t have trust — you have a performance. When did someone on your team last push back on you directly, in the room? If you can’t remember, that’s your answer.

Why This Matters

Leaders without honest feedback fly blind. Psychological safety isn’t a soft HR initiative — it’s the engine that powers everything else.

2
Question Two

Am I developing my people, or just using them?

There’s a version of leadership that looks productive but is quietly extractive. You get results, but when someone leaves, everything wobbles — because you were getting output, not building capacity.

If you left tomorrow, which of your people would be ready to do parts of your job? If the answer is no one, you’re running, not leading.

Why This Matters

The best leaders make themselves partially obsolete on purpose. If your team only performs when you’re watching, you haven’t led them. You’ve managed them.

3
Question Three

Do I hold the same standard for myself that I hold for everyone else?

Most leaders believe they do — and most don’t. The gap between the standard you hold others to and the one you hold yourself to is the most watched gap in any organization. Your team sees it even when you don’t.

What’s the last standard you held someone else to that you quietly broke yourself last month?

Why This Matters

Culture is not what you say you value. It’s what you tolerate — starting with what you tolerate from yourself.

4
Question Four

What am I avoiding, and why?

Every leader has conversations they keep rescheduling and decisions waiting on more information. Avoidance is not a personal failure — it’s a signal. Discomfort is directional. It points toward the terrain you haven’t figured out yet.

What’s the one thing that keeps appearing on your mental to-do list that you keep moving to next week?

Why This Matters

Most leadership breakdowns are slow-motion. They start as avoidances. Catching your own early stops the slow burn before it becomes a fire.

5
Question Five

Is the way I lead costing people something I don’t know about?

This is the hardest question — because it requires you to consider that your leadership style, even when it’s working, may be extracting a cost you aren’t seeing. Leadership has externalities. How you run meetings, give feedback, and show up on your bad days has downstream effects on real people.

If your three hardest-to-lead team members were honest with you right now, what would they say your leadership is costing them?

Why This Matters

The leaders who grow fastest treat this as a standing agenda item, not a one-time reflection. Asking regularly — and creating the conditions where people can actually answer — is one of the most consequential things a leader can do.
